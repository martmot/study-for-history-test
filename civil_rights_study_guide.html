Core Concept Map

Every major topic shown as Cause → Mechanism → Effect. Understanding the chain of causation is more valuable than memorizing isolated facts.

Ch. 27Truman's Civil Rights Actions (1948)
Cause
Rising Black political power in northern cities + Cold War need to appear democratic to non-white nations globally
→
Mechanism
Truman desegregates military (Exec. Order 9981); proposes anti-lynching law, poll tax repeal, end to employment discrimination
→
Effect
Dixiecrats bolt party; Strom Thurmond runs as States' Rights candidate; seeds planted for full civil rights confrontation
Ch. 27Eisenhower's "Middle of the Road" Approach
Cause
Eisenhower wanted to keep both moderate liberals and conservative Southern Democrats within his coalition
→
Mechanism
Avoided strong civil rights legislation; sent troops to Little Rock (1957) to enforce federal law — not out of conviction but to preserve federal authority
→
Effect
His reluctance emboldened Southern resistance; structural forces (federal supremacy, Cold War) produced civil rights outcomes without a committed president
Ch. 29Brown v. Board of Education (1954)
Cause
NAACP legal strategy (Thurgood Marshall) dismantling the "separate but equal" doctrine established by Plessy v. Ferguson (1896)
→
Mechanism
Supreme Court unanimously rules school segregation unconstitutional; removes legal justification for Jim Crow laws broadly
→
Effect
Massive resistance in South (esp. Arkansas); deep political polarization; catalyst for dismantling further Jim Crow laws nationwide
Ch. 29MLK, SCLC & Nonviolent Civil Disobedience
Cause
Gandhi's nonviolence model + Christian ethics ("love thy neighbor") + de jure segregation in the South demanding organized resistance
→
Mechanism
SCLC organizes boycotts, sit-ins, freedom rides, marches; March on Washington (1963) demands federal legislation before 250,000 people
→
Effect
Civil Rights Act (1964) outlaws public discrimination; Voting Rights Act (1965) bans literacy tests and mandates federal election oversight
Ch. 29De Jure vs. De Facto Segregation & Urban Crisis
Cause
Civil Rights Act (1964) & Voting Rights Act (1965) attacked legal discrimination but left redlining, unemployment, and housing inequality untouched in Northern cities
→
Mechanism
Northern Black communities faced de facto segregation — structural economic discrimination invisible to new laws; frustration boiled over
→
Effect
Race riots mid-1960s (Detroit, Newark, Watts); rise of Malcolm X, Black Power, Black Panthers demanding economic justice not just legal equality
Ch. 31Latino & Farm Worker Movement (UFW)
Cause
Exploitation of Latino agricultural laborers; denial of basic labor protections; lack of political voice or land rights
→
Mechanism
César Chávez leads UFW using nonviolent strikes, grape boycotts, fasting; La Raza Unida drives voter registration; Chicano Movement promotes cultural pride
→
Effect
Improved wages and working conditions; growing Latino political representation; lasting cultural pride movement across Southwest
Ch. 31Native Americans & AIM
Cause
Forced assimilation policies, broken treaties, poverty on reservations, destruction of tribal culture across decades
→
Mechanism
American Indian Movement (AIM) uses urban aid programs, dramatic protests (Wounded Knee 1973), legal challenges to assert treaty rights and sovereignty
→
Effect
Advancements in Native sovereignty, treaty rights recognition, and cultural revitalization; Indian Self-Determination Act (1975)

Cause

→
Mechanism

→

Cause
Legal equality achieved but economic inequality persisted; JFK Executive Order 10925 (1961) introduced proactive equal opportunity requirements
→
Mechanism
Federal contractors required to actively recruit minorities and women; universities adopt affirmative admissions policies to diversify campuses
→

Political controversy and legal challenges; some gains in representation; persistent debate over whether it addresses root inequality or creates new unfairness

Deep Analysis

7 "hidden" connections that won't appear directly in your notes but are very likely to appear on high-level exams. These are the insights that separate a B from an A.

Inference 01
The Cold War was a hidden engine of civil rights progress
The U.S. competed globally against the Soviet Union for the loyalty of newly independent, often non-white nations in Africa and Asia. Segregated schools and lynchings were propaganda weapons the USSR deployed against America. This gave Truman and Eisenhower strategic geopolitical reasons — beyond moral conviction — to act on civil rights. When an exam asks "why did change happen when it did?" always consider Cold War optics alongside domestic pressure.
Inference 02
Legal equality ≠ economic equality — this gap explains the entire urban crisis
The Civil Rights Act (1964) and Voting Rights Act (1965) dismantled de jure (legal) discrimination. But redlining, unemployment, underfunded schools, and housing segregation — de facto discrimination — remained completely untouched by either law. This is the single most important reason race riots erupted in Northern cities AFTER the movement's greatest legal victories. The law changed the rules; it didn't change the economic game. This insight appears on virtually every high-level exam.
Inference 03
Malcolm X and MLK were complements, not opposites
A common oversimplification: Malcolm X = violent, MLK = peaceful. More accurately, Malcolm X's assertive rhetoric made MLK seem like a reasonable, moderate negotiating partner to white politicians — creating political space for legislative compromise. Every social movement benefits from both a "radical flank" that shifts the Overton window and a negotiating voice that closes the deal. Exam questions often ask: "How did more militant groups impact mainstream civil rights gains?" This dynamic is the answer.
Inference 04
All 1960s social movements shared a single tactical playbook
African Americans, Latinos (UFW), Native Americans (AIM), and women all employed strikingly similar methods: nonviolent direct action, grassroots organizing, legal challenges, and strategic media exposure. This was not coincidence — these movements actively learned from and inspired each other. Chávez explicitly modeled UFW tactics on the civil rights movement. On exams, you may be asked to compare tactics across groups or explain why the 1960s produced so many simultaneous movements.
Inference 05
The ERA's failure shaped American politics more than its passage would have
The defeat of the Equal Rights Amendment galvanized Phyllis Schlafly's conservative women's movement and directly contributed to the rise of the New Right under Reagan. The ERA debate forced Americans to publicly define gender roles, and the backlash it generated reshaped the Republican Party for decades. A failed law can be a bigger historical turning point than a passed one — the political mobilization it triggered outlasted the movement that pushed it.
Inference 06
Eisenhower's reluctance paradoxically strengthened Southern resistance
Because Eisenhower never publicly embraced school desegregation and delayed federal action, Southern officials believed they could resist indefinitely. He sent troops to Little Rock in 1957 only to enforce federal authority — not from personal conviction. His lack of moral leadership signaled to segregationists that they had room to maneuver, making the confrontations of the early 1960s more intense than they might otherwise have been. Leadership's moral clarity (or lack thereof) shapes how opponents calculate their options.
Inference 07
Reactive legislation (post-tragedy) is systematically weaker
The Civil Rights Act of 1968 (Fair Housing Act) was passed in the immediate aftermath of MLK's assassination and the riots it triggered — Congress acting under emotional pressure rather than sustained political will. Reactive laws passed to calm crises typically lack strong enforcement mechanisms. The pattern: tragedy → political shock → hasty legislation → weak enforcement → persistent inequality. This explains why the Fair Housing Act did not meaningfully integrate American housing despite its bold language.

Watch Out Annex

The most strategic section. Read this the night before your exam.

⭐ Top 5 High-Yield Facts to Master
1
De jure vs. de facto segregation — definitions AND examples, cold
De jure = legal segregation (Jim Crow laws, poll taxes, literacy tests). De facto = segregation in practice without explicit laws (redlining, concentrated poverty, underfunded schools in Northern cities). The exam WILL ask you to distinguish these.
2
Three separate civil rights laws: 1964, 1965, and 1968 — different content, different impact
1964 Civil Rights Act: banned discrimination in public accommodations and employment. 1965 Voting Rights Act: ended literacy tests, provided federal oversight of elections. 1968 Fair Housing Act: banned housing discrimination. Know what each did — and what it DIDN'T solve.
3
Why urban riots erupted AFTER the legal victories of 1964–65
Because neither act addressed economic inequality, unemployment, or housing discrimination in Northern cities. Laws changed the legal framework; they didn't change material conditions. This is the central theme of post-1965 civil rights history.
4
Leaders AND their philosophies: MLK, Malcolm X, Chávez, AIM, Friedan, NOW
Don't just memorize names. Know: MLK = nonviolence + integration. Malcolm X = Black nationalism + self-defense. Chávez = nonviolent labor action. AIM = sovereignty + treaty rights. Friedan = "The Feminine Mystique" + NOW. Each leader's philosophy explains their tactics.
5
Affirmative Action origin: JFK Exec. Order 10925 (1961) — before the Civil Rights Act
Affirmative action was proactive, not just reactive. It required federal contractors to take "affirmative action" to ensure equal opportunity — meaning actively recruit minorities, not just stop discriminating. This distinction between proactive and reactive policy appears on advanced exams.
⚠️ Trap Library — 5 Trick Questions
Trap 1 — The Scope Trap
"The Civil Rights Act of 1964 ended segregation everywhere in the U.S."
Wrong. It ended de jure (legal) segregation primarily in the South. De facto segregation in Northern cities persisted entirely through economic mechanisms untouched by the law. This is exactly why riots erupted in Northern cities despite the Act's passage. The trap: assuming a law that changes rules also changes realities.
Trap 2 — The Stereotype Trap
"Malcolm X was a violent extremist" or "The Black Panthers were a terrorist group"
Oversimplified. Malcolm X advocated self-defense against violence, not aggression. The Black Panthers ran community programs — free breakfasts for children, health clinics — alongside their militant rhetoric. Exams may present them as simply violent to see if you offer nuanced analysis of their actual goals, programs, and historical context.
Trap 3 — The Eisenhower Trap
"Eisenhower was a supporter of civil rights because he sent troops to Little Rock"
He was personally reluctant. He took a "middle of the road" approach specifically to avoid alienating Southern conservatives. He sent troops to Little Rock (1957) to uphold federal authority over state law — not from moral conviction about desegregation. His private views and public administrative duties were very different. Don't conflate administrative action with ideological commitment.
Trap 4 — The Three Laws Confusion Trap
Questions that mix up what year/what the 1964, 1965, and 1968 laws did
There are THREE major civil rights laws. Many students merge them into one vague "Civil Rights Act." 1964 = public places + employment. 1965 = voting rights (literacy tests). 1968 = fair housing. Exams will give you a specific issue and ask which law addressed it — or trick you by asking about housing discrimination and seeing if you say "1964."
Trap 5 — The ERA Success Trap
"The women's movement was successful — the ERA became law"
The ERA FAILED ratification. Congress passed it in 1972, but it was never ratified by enough states. Phyllis Schlafly's conservative campaign argued it would eliminate military service exemptions and alimony protections for women. Women gained real legal gains (Title IX, equal pay progress, political representation) but the ERA itself died. Always distinguish what the movement accomplished from what its specific legislative goal achieved.

Key Timeline

The chronological backbone. Knowing when events happened relative to each other reveals cause-and-effect chains that isolated memorization misses.

1896
Plessy v. Ferguson — "Separate But Equal"
Supreme Court establishes legal foundation for Jim Crow segregation. This ruling stands for nearly 60 years and is the target of the entire NAACP legal strategy.
1948
Truman's Executive Order 9981
Desegregates the U.S. military. Dixiecrats bolt the Democratic Party; Strom Thurmond runs as States' Rights candidate, winning 4 Southern states.
1954
Brown v. Board of Education
Supreme Court unanimously overturns Plessy. Chief Justice Earl Warren writes that "separate educational facilities are inherently unequal." Massive Southern resistance follows.
1955–56
Montgomery Bus Boycott
Sparked by Rosa Parks's arrest. MLK emerges as national leader. The 381-day boycott succeeds; Supreme Court rules bus segregation unconstitutional.
1957
Little Rock Crisis
Arkansas Governor Faubus uses National Guard to block Black students from Central High School. Eisenhower sends 101st Airborne — not from conviction, but to enforce federal law over state defiance.
1961
JFK Executive Order 10925 — Affirmative Action
First official use of "affirmative action." Federal contractors required to take proactive steps to ensure equal employment opportunity regardless of race, creed, color, or national origin.
1963
March on Washington — "I Have a Dream"
250,000 people march in D.C. MLK delivers "I Have a Dream" speech. The march directly pressures Congress toward civil rights legislation. Four girls killed in 16th Street Baptist Church bombing two weeks later.
1964
Civil Rights Act of 1964
Outlaws discrimination in public accommodations and employment on the basis of race, color, religion, sex, or national origin. Addresses de jure discrimination in the South but does not touch Northern economic inequality.
1965
Voting Rights Act of 1965
Bans literacy tests and other voter suppression devices. Provides for federal examiners in areas with histories of discrimination. Directly follows Bloody Sunday at Selma's Edmund Pettus Bridge.
1965–68
Urban Race Riots — Watts, Detroit, Newark
Riots erupt in Northern cities despite legal gains. Root causes: unemployment, redlining, police brutality, de facto segregation. The Kerner Commission warns America is "moving toward two societies, one black, one white."
1966
Black Power Movement & Black Panthers Founded
Stokely Carmichael popularizes "Black Power." Black Panther Party founded in Oakland by Huey Newton and Bobby Seale — emphasis on armed self-defense, community programs, and economic independence.
1968
MLK Assassinated + Civil Rights Act of 1968
MLK killed in Memphis. Nationwide riots follow. Congress passes Fair Housing Act within a week — reactive legislation under emotional pressure. The Act bans housing discrimination but is weakly enforced.
1972
ERA Passes Congress / Title IX Enacted
Equal Rights Amendment passes Congress, sent to states for ratification. Title IX bans gender discrimination in federally funded education — the women's movement's most durable legislative achievement.
1972–82
ERA Ratification Battle & Failure
Phyllis Schlafly's STOP ERA campaign defeats ratification. ERA falls 3 states short. Its defeat galvanizes conservative women and directly contributes to the rise of the New Right under Reagan.
1973
Wounded Knee Occupation — AIM
American Indian Movement occupies Wounded Knee, South Dakota for 71 days, demanding U.S. honor 1868 Fort Laramie Treaty. National media attention advances Native sovereignty issues.
